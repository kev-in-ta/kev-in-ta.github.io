MECH328

Backcountry Ski Ascender

Biomechanics assist tool for backcountry ski ascension

Third Year Project Course

biomedical design analysis parts spec.

MECH 328 is the senior design project where we are tasked with a design problem and must perform an in-depth design analysis. The challenge was to design a device to aid back-country skier ascend terrain more easily and to maximize the amount of skiing the user could accomplish in one day. My team designed a flexible biomechanical assistance device which aids the hip flexor motion. Strapped to your back, this device aids lifting your leg upward for each step, reducing the amount of energy needed for the user to ascend slopes.

I performed:

  • Biomechanical modelling of the forces acting in backcountry skiing
  • Analysis on additional support without impeding the user during downhill descent
  • Extensive design to set-up the device to be ready for future in-depth prototyping with all mechanisms specified and expect

Frontal view of backcountry ski ascension

Biomechanical Analysis

The biomechanics analysis found:

  • The hip flexor as the muscle in most need of assistance
  • Hip flexors were not designed to lift heavy loads beyond the weight of the leg and are often the weakest link for backcountry skiers.

Mechanical Advantage

One of the key attributes of providing assistance is proper mechanical advantage. We can achieve this by:

  • Positioning the pulley system above the hip to provide greater mechanical advantage compared to the low torque arm provided by a typical hip flexor.
  • A clutch mechanism that allows for assistance when needed and slack when not
  • Adjusting to the user’s stride, allowing aid to both experienced and novice backcountry skiers

Design-in-Detail

As a in depth design course, a number of attributes were explored and presented in a comprehensive report.

This report included:

  • General engineering analysis, with calculations, research, and the final design assembly, including detailed engineering drawings
  • A cost estimate with all components specified such that the vested party (the course instructors) would be able to evaluate the economic viability

Producing this report took the expertise of a team of six students with specific strengths within mechanical design, allowing a thorough analysis of a complex system.

Scale Regenerative Braking Vehicle

Small-scale mechanically-autonomous rengenerative vehicle

Second Year Project Course

design rapid prototyping kinematics arduino

The first project in the MECH2 project course was to develop a regenerative braking vehicle. This vehicle must convert potential energy into some stored energy, brake, and then re-release that energy to move forward.

This project involves:

  • Considering various aspects such as size, weight, and performance all affect the final result
  • Developing a comprehensive design report to justify every decision to simulate a larger real-world engineering project

Final Design

The key aspect of the design is how to store and release energy. Our design incorporates:

  • A torsional spring to store and release energy for braking
  • A gear switching system that can change from forward spin to reverse spin, allowing for the spring to wind in one direction and release in the other (this factor was a primary concern due to the nature of how springs store and release energy in opposite directions)
  • A rotary encoder was used to track distance and speed to control braking

MECH220

Magnetic Levitator

Student-fabricated on magnetic levitator

Fabrication & Design Fundamentals

machining soldering drawings tolerances

MECH 220 is the second year introduction course covering design fundamentals for mechanical engineers. The material covered in the class include hand drafting and dimensioning, 3D modelling, machining, and instrumentation. These skills are put to use to develop a magnetic levitator which keeps a rod floating using a feedback controlled solenoid.

Fabrication

  • Many of the parts, such as the steel housing, were constructed using lathes to form parts as accurately as five thousandth of an inch of precision. Supports were made form hollow steel tubes.
  • Technical drawings of many of the major parts were also constructed and used to coordinate tolerances on project parts.
  • The final aspect was the electronics portion of the mag-lev. rom multiple feedback operational amplifiers and a variable potentiometer, the resulting magnetic force could be modified so the feedback strength kept the rod levitating.
